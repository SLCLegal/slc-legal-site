Common matters

Tell us what happened, not what label to use.

Many clients come in with overlapping issues: retaliation after a medical leave request, lost wages after a workplace dispute, emotional injury after harassment, or injury-related disruption that reaches far beyond the original event. You do not need to classify everything perfectly before reaching out.

Retaliation and wrongful termination You reported a problem, requested leave, asked for accommodation, raised pay issues, or asserted your rights, and the employer turned against you.
Discrimination and harassment Pregnancy, disability, medical condition, race, national origin, religion, sex, gender identity or expression, sexual orientation, or age changed how you were treated.
Wage and hour Unpaid overtime, salary misclassification, missed breaks, late final pay, inaccurate wage statements, or broader pay practices affecting groups of workers.
Leave and accommodation You requested medical leave, schedule changes, restrictions, or accommodation, and the response was delay, denial, punishment, or pressure.
Whistleblower and Labor Code retaliation You reported unlawful conduct, safety concerns, fraud, patient-care issues, or wage violations and were disciplined, forced out, or terminated.
Personal injury Auto collisions, premises liability, and other injury matters where treatment, proof, timing, preservation, and damages need to be handled carefully from the outset.

Not every case fits into a single box.

Some matters begin as employment disputes and develop into medical, financial, and emotional crises. Others begin with a physical injury and quickly become income, treatment, and documentation problems. Strong representation requires understanding the full chain of harm.

  • Workplace pressure and retaliation can produce real emotional and economic damage
  • Medical issues and treatment history often matter in both employment and injury contexts
  • Serious cases are shaped by chronology, records, witnesses, and credibility from the start
  • Early intake should preserve the facts before they get diluted, reframed, or lost
